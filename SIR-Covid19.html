Theory¶

In epidemiology, the SIR-model is a simple compartmental model that can be applied to an outbreak like the Corona-pandemic.

This model distinguishes Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered (or Removed) fractions of the population. Susceptible people can get infected, and infected persons eventually recover (or die), but do not become infected again.

  • The fraction that newly catches the disease is proportional to both the available susceptible fraction that can acquire the disease ($S$) and the infected fraction that can transfer the disease ($I$): $[S \rightarrow I] = \alpha \cdot S \cdot I$, where $\alpha$ is the infection rate.

  • The population fraction that recovers (or dies) is proportional to only the fraction of infected people itself ($I$): $[I \rightarrow R] = \beta \cdot I$, where $\beta$ is the recovery rate.

Thus, we obtain a set of differential equations

$$ \begin{aligned} \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}S &= -\alpha \cdot S \cdot I \\ \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}I &= +\alpha \cdot S \cdot I - \beta \cdot I \\ \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}R &= +\beta \cdot I \end{aligned} $$

Because $S$, $I$, and $R$ are fractions, $0 \leq S,I,R \leq 1$, and $S+I+R=1$.

For Covid-19, estimated infection and recovery rates are not well known, but during the initial phases of the pandemic these amounted to approximately $\alpha = 0.25$ day-1 and $\beta = 0.10$ day-1.

Step 1: Modelling recovery¶

Starting from a hypothetical population that is 100% infected, we can model the recovery of patients by calculating for each day how many people recover, and transferring these from the Infected to the Recovered fraction.

We maintain lists to keep track of the numbers each day.

In [3]:
I = 1.000
R = 0.000

Ilist = []
Rlist = []

for day in range(150):
    
    Ilist.append(I)
    Rlist.append(R)
    
    recoveries = beta * I
    I -= recoveries
    R += recoveries

Next we plot the results. We see that the infected population gradually recovers according to an exponential relationship.

plt.xlabel('$t$ [days]'); plt.ylabel('$f$ [-]')
plt.legend(); plt.show()

Step 2: Modelling infections¶

Now it is easy to add infections in a similar fashion. Below, we start with a predominantly healthy but susceptible population with a tiny fraction of 1 infected people.

In [5]:
S = 0.999
I = 0.001
R = 0.000

Slist = []
Ilist = []
Rlist = []

for day in range(150):
    
    Slist.append(S)
    Ilist.append(I)
    Rlist.append(R)
    
    infections = alpha * S * I
    S -= infections
    I += infections
    
    recoveries = beta * I
    I -= recoveries
    R += recoveries

And again we plot the results. Now we see that the number of infected patients first increases, but then decreases again. When the pandemic is over, approximately 90% of the population has been infected but recovered at some time, whereas 10% never got infected at all.

Step 3: Numerical integration of differential equations¶

The chosen approach is somewhat inaccurate. For instance, we determine the number of patients day-by-day, but this is inaccurate since the number of patients will change over the course of that day. More advanced methods to compute a more accurate solution are available. We can exploit some standard functions from the scipy module to achieve this.

The solve_ivp() function requires the differential equation to be provided in the form of a single function. Therefore, we need to combine the $S$, $I$, and $R$ values into a three-element vector-quantity $\boldsymbol{v} = \left[ S, I, R \right]^\text{T}$. The three differential equations can then be combined into a single equation:

$$ \frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}t}\boldsymbol{v} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} -\alpha \cdot v_1 \cdot v_2 \\ +\alpha \cdot v_1 \cdot v_2 - \beta \cdot v_2 \\ +\beta \cdot v_2 \end{array} \right] $$
In [7]:
# Define model
def SIRmodel(t, v):
    return [
            - alpha * v[0] * v[1],
            + alpha * v[0] * v[1] - beta * v[1],
            + beta * v[1]
           ]

We furthermore provide the initial values and time points that we wish to calculate, and run the integration. The resulting plot looks almost (but not exactly) the same compared to the solution we found with our crude stepwise integration.

In [9]:
fromto = (0.0, 150.0)
start = [0.999, 0.001, 0.000]
times = np.linspace(0.0, 150.0, 151)

solution = solve_ivp(SIRmodel, fromto, start, t_eval=times)

plt.plot(solution.t, solution.y[0], '-y', label='Susceptible')
plt.plot(solution.t, solution.y[1], '-r', label='Infected')
plt.plot(solution.t, solution.y[2], '-g', label='Recovered')
plt.xlabel('$t$ [days]'); plt.ylabel('$f$ [-]')
plt.legend(); plt.show()
